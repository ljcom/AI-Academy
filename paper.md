From Knowledge Engines to AI Academy Engines:
Rethinking Conversational AI for Learning Progression
Abstract
Recent advances in conversational artificial intelligence have transformed AI systems into powerful knowledge engines capable of answering a wide range of learner questions. While this unprecedented accessibility of information offers new opportunities for self-directed learning, it also introduces challenges related to superficial understanding and the illusion of mastery. Conversational interactions with AI often follow a simple question–answer pattern that does not necessarily lead to structured learning progression.
This chapter proposes the concept of an AI Academy Engine, a framework that reimagines conversational AI not merely as an information provider but as a guided learning system. In this approach, learners begin with a common conversational entry point and are guided through adaptive learning pathways, including dialogue-based exploration, real-world observation, visual explanation, or problem-solving challenges. The AI system supports reflective dialogue, evaluates conceptual understanding, and determines readiness for progression.
The proposed framework highlights the potential of conversational AI to evolve from a knowledge access tool into a structured learning environment supporting mastery-based learning. Ethical considerations related to learner data, cognitive profiling, and privacy are also discussed.

1 Introduction
The rapid development of conversational artificial intelligence has significantly expanded access to knowledge. Modern large language models are capable of generating explanations, answering complex questions, and assisting learners in exploring a wide variety of topics. In many contexts, AI systems now function as powerful knowledge engines, capable of providing instant information on demand.
This transformation has created new possibilities for self-directed learning. Learners can now interact with AI systems to clarify concepts, explore unfamiliar subjects, and receive explanations tailored to their questions. However, this unprecedented accessibility of information also introduces new challenges for education.
One of the key concerns is that the availability of instant answers may not necessarily lead to deeper learning (Bloom, 1984; Flavell, 1979). Conversational AI interactions often follow a simple pattern:
question → answer → topic shift
In this interaction model, learners may obtain explanations but do not necessarily engage in reflection, conceptual validation, or application of knowledge. As a result, learners may develop an illusion of understanding, believing they have mastered a topic without demonstrating deeper comprehension (Rozenblit & Keil, 2002).
Traditional educational systems address this challenge through structured learning progression. In academic environments, advancement typically depends on demonstrated mastery through exercises, reflection, and evaluation. In contrast, conversational AI environments rarely incorporate mechanisms that support learning progression or mastery verification.
This chapter explores how conversational AI could evolve beyond its current role as an answer provider. It introduces the concept of an AI Academy Engine, a framework designed to transform conversational AI into a guided learning environment capable of supporting mastery-based learning progression.

2 Limitations of Current Conversational AI Learning
Conversational AI systems have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in supporting informal learning and intelligent tutoring interactions (VanLehn, 2011). Learners can ask questions in natural language and receive detailed explanations across a wide range of subjects. Despite these advantages, several limitations emerge when conversational AI is used as a primary learning tool.
First, conversational AI interactions depend heavily on the learner’s ability to formulate meaningful questions. Not all learners possess the same capacity to identify knowledge gaps or articulate relevant inquiries. As a result, learners who are less confident in asking questions may experience shallow interactions with AI systems.
Second, conversational AI systems typically do not verify whether learners have truly understood a concept. The interaction often ends once an explanation has been delivered. Without mechanisms for reflection or application, the learner’s comprehension remains uncertain.
Third, conversational learning environments lack structured progression. In traditional education, learners move through carefully designed sequences of concepts and exercises. In contrast, AI-driven interactions may jump between topics without ensuring that foundational understanding has been established.
Finally, conversational AI systems rarely consider differences in learner preferences or learning styles. Some learners may benefit from dialogue-based exploration, while others prefer observation, visual explanation, or practical experimentation.
These limitations suggest that conversational AI alone may not be sufficient to support structured learning progression.

3 Learning Progression in Traditional Education
In formal education, learning is typically organized around structured progression. Students advance through levels of understanding by demonstrating mastery of key concepts and skills, a principle central to mastery learning theory (Bloom, 1984).
A simplified representation of this progression is:
concept introduction → practice → reflection → mastery → progression
In this model, learners do not advance merely by receiving explanations. Instead, they must demonstrate the ability to apply knowledge, explain concepts, and solve related problems. This process ensures that learning involves both cognitive engagement and conceptual understanding.
The structured nature of academic learning environments contrasts with the open-ended nature of conversational AI systems. While AI provides immediate access to knowledge, it does not inherently support progression through stages of mastery.
This gap suggests the need for a new learning framework that combines the flexibility of conversational AI with the structured progression found in traditional educational systems.

Conversational AI and the Shift in Learning Interaction
The emergence of conversational AI represents a fundamental shift in how learners interact with knowledge systems. Traditionally, digital learning environments have relied on static content formats such as textbooks, lecture slides, or recorded videos. These materials present information in a largely one-directional manner, where learners consume content and later demonstrate understanding through exercises or examinations.

Conversational AI introduces a different interaction paradigm. Instead of passively receiving information, learners can engage in dynamic dialogue with an AI system capable of responding to questions, generating explanations, and adapting responses to the learner’s level of understanding. This interaction creates the possibility of a more personalized learning experience.

However, while conversational interaction increases accessibility and flexibility, it does not automatically guarantee deeper learning. Without structured guidance, learners may move quickly between topics without developing a coherent conceptual framework. As a result, conversational AI must be complemented with mechanisms that support structured learning progression.


4 Concept of the AI Academy Engine
To address the limitations of conversational AI learning, this chapter proposes the concept of an AI Academy Engine.
An AI Academy Engine extends the role of conversational AI beyond answering questions. Instead, the AI system acts as a learning progression guide, helping learners move through stages of exploration, reflection, and mastery.

In this framework, AI evolves from an answer machine into a learning progression engine.

Unlike traditional classroom structures that often progress through a single topic sequence, the AI Academy Engine supports multi-topic and multi-level learning progression. Learners may explore several related topics simultaneously, advancing more quickly in areas where conceptual understanding develops faster while remaining at earlier stages in topics that require deeper reflection. This flexible structure allows learners to reach higher conceptual levels in certain domains while continuing to strengthen foundations in others.

Importantly, temporary stagnation at a particular level is not interpreted as failure. Instead, learning progress can be monitored by mentors or educators who oversee the AI-supported environment. If a learner remains at a specific conceptual stage for an extended period, mentors may intervene by adjusting the learning pathway, introducing alternative explanations, or encouraging different learning activities such as observation, experimentation, or collaborative discussion. In this way, learning progression becomes adaptive rather than strictly linear, reducing the traditional notion of "failing to advance" and replacing it with continuous guided development.

5 Conversational Entry and Adaptive Learning Pathways
In the AI Academy Engine framework, all learners begin with a common conversational entry point. The AI system initiates dialogue to assess the learner’s existing understanding of a topic.
For example, the AI may begin with a prompt such as:
“What do you already know about this topic?”

Based on the learner’s response, the AI can guide the learning process through several adaptive pathways.
Possible learning pathways include:

- **Dialogue-based exploration** – Learners engage in conceptual discussion with the AI through guided questions.
- **Observation-based learning** – Learners observe real-world examples and return to discuss their observations with the AI.
- **Visual exploration** – The AI provides diagrams, simulations, or visual explanations of concepts.
- **Problem-solving challenges** – Learners apply knowledge through case studies or practical scenarios.
This approach allows learners to select learning pathways that align with their preferences while maintaining a common conceptual objective.

Diversity of Learning Preferences

Another important consideration in conversational learning environments is the diversity of learner preferences. Students differ in how they engage with knowledge and process new information. While some learners enjoy extended dialogue and conceptual discussion, others prefer learning through observation, experimentation, or visual representation.
For example, reflective learners may benefit significantly from conversational interaction with AI, where ideas are explored through dialogue and guided questioning. In contrast, more action-oriented learners may find purely conversational learning environments less engaging. These learners may prefer practical tasks, real-world observation, or problem-solving challenges.
The AI Academy Engine framework addresses this diversity by offering multiple learning pathways. Instead of imposing a single instructional method, the system allows learners to select pathways that align with their learning preferences while still guiding them toward the same conceptual objectives.

6 Reflective Dialogue and Mastery Evaluation
A key component of the AI Academy Engine is reflective dialogue.
Rather than simply providing explanations, the AI prompts learners to reflect on their understanding. For example, learners may be asked to:

- Explain concepts in their own words
- Analyze hypothetical scenarios
- Compare alternative explanations
- Apply concepts to real-world situations

Through these interactions, the AI can assess indicators of conceptual understanding.
Learning progression may follow stages such as:
exploration → reflection → validation → mastery → progression
When the AI determines that a learner has achieved sufficient conceptual clarity, the learner may advance to the next level of study.

AI as a Question Generator

An important limitation of many conversational learning environments is their dependence on learner-initiated questions. Not all learners possess the confidence or ability to formulate meaningful questions, particularly when encountering unfamiliar topics.

To address this limitation, the AI Academy Engine incorporates an active questioning mechanism. Rather than waiting for learner questions, the AI system initiates dialogue through guided prompts designed to stimulate reflection and reasoning.

This approach draws inspiration from the Socratic method, in which learning occurs through carefully structured questioning and reflective dialogue (Paul & Elder, 2007). By asking learners to explain concepts, evaluate scenarios, or compare alternative explanations, the AI system can encourage deeper cognitive engagement.

In this context, AI functions not only as an answer provider but also as a question generator that facilitates reflective learning.

7 Ethical and Privacy Considerations
The implementation of AI-driven learning environments raises important ethical considerations.
AI Academy Engines may analyse learner interactions, response patterns, and cognitive behaviour in order to assess learning progression. While such data can improve personalized learning, it also raises concerns regarding privacy and cognitive profiling.
Educational institutions adopting such systems must therefore ensure:

- Transparent data collection policies
- Informed learner consent
- Strict protection of learning data
- Responsible use of behavioral analytics

Maintaining trust and safeguarding learner autonomy are essential for the ethical deployment of AI-based learning systems.

Illustrative Learning Scenario

To illustrate the proposed framework, consider a learner studying the concept of supply chain systems.

The learning process begins with a conversational entry point where the AI asks the learner about their existing knowledge. Based on the learner’s response, the AI offers several learning pathways.

For example, the learner may choose to explore the concept through dialogue, observation of real-world examples, or problem-solving challenges. If the learner selects the observation pathway, the AI may suggest observing how products are distributed in a local store or how restaurants receive their supplies.

After completing the observation, the learner returns to the AI system to discuss their findings. The AI then guides the learner through reflective dialogue, helping them connect their observations with theoretical concepts.

Through this iterative process, the learner gradually develops a deeper understanding of supply chain systems while engaging with both conceptual and experiential learning activities.

8 Conclusion
Conversational AI systems have transformed access to knowledge by enabling learners to obtain explanations instantly. However, access to information alone does not guarantee meaningful learning progression.
This chapter has introduced the concept of the AI Academy Engine, a framework that reimagines conversational AI as a guided learning environment rather than a simple answer provider. By incorporating adaptive learning pathways, reflective dialogue, and mastery evaluation, AI systems could support more structured learning experiences.
As conversational AI technologies continue to evolve, the challenge for educational technology will not be merely to provide answers but to design systems that guide learners through meaningful journeys of understanding.

Future Research Directions

The concept of the AI Academy Engine opens several directions for future research.

First, further investigation is needed to understand how conversational AI systems can accurately evaluate conceptual understanding without relying solely on traditional assessments. Developing reliable indicators of mastery within conversational dialogue remains an open challenge.

Second, research is needed to explore how AI systems can detect learner preferences and adapt instructional strategies accordingly, building on earlier work in adaptive learning systems (Brusilovsky, 2001). Advances in learner modeling may enable AI systems to identify patterns in learner behavior and dynamically adjust learning pathways.

Finally, future work should examine the long-term impact of AI-guided learning progression on student motivation, knowledge retention, and metacognitive awareness.
